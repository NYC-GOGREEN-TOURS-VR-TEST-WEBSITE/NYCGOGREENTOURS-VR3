The Bronx in the 1970s — “The Bronx Is Burning”

Entire neighborhoods burned. Landlords torched buildings for insurance. Firehouses closed. President Carter visited Charlotte Street in 1977 to witness the devastation. These images show the reality of the era.

The Bronx in the 1980s — Abandonment & Survival

Crack hit the Bronx. Gangs controlled blocks. Buildings stood hollow and empty. President Reagan and later President Clinton visited to see the destruction. These images show the Bronx at its lowest point.

The Rebirth Begins — The New Yankee Stadium

The construction of the new Yankee Stadium (2006–2009) brought billions in investment, new parks, new roads, and new transit. This project sparked the modern Bronx renaissance.

Bronx Terminal Market — 145th to 149th Street

The redevelopment of the Bronx Terminal Market cleaned up the Harlem River waterfront, replaced abandoned warehouses, and created a major retail hub that stabilized the entire area.

138th Street Corridor — The New Gateway

Once filled with burned lots and empty buildings, the 138th Street corridor is now lined with renovated pre‑war buildings, new apartments, restaurants, and families. This is the gateway to the new South Bronx.

149th & 3rd Avenue — The Hub

Once one of the most dangerous intersections in New York City, The Hub is now a major transit center surrounded by retail, housing, and community development.

Hunts Point — From Fish Market to Economic Engine

The relocation of the old Fulton Fish Market to Hunts Point brought jobs, infrastructure, and federal investment, turning the area into the industrial heart of the Bronx.

Grand Concourse — 161st to 183rd Street

The Grand Concourse is undergoing massive renovation. Art Deco buildings are being restored, new construction is rising, and neighborhoods like Tremont and Katonah Park east of Webster are transforming rapidly.

The Full Transformation

From the fires of the 1970s to the rebirth of the 2000s, the South Bronx has undergone one of the greatest urban transformations in American history. This tour shows the past, the present, and the future — through the eyes of someone who lived it.
